Research Themes

What We Study

A multi-disciplinary approach spanning molecular microbiology, AMR transmission, and compound discovery.

01
Horizontal Gene Transfer

Investigating how resistance genes spread between bacterial populations via plasmids, and how environmental pressures accelerate this exchange.

02
Resistance Mechanisms

Deciphering the molecular machinery bacteria use to evade antibiotics — to identify exploitable vulnerabilities.

03
Biofilm & Persistence

Understanding how bacterial biofilms and persister cells survive antibiotic challenge, creating reservoirs of recalcitrant infection and enabling the evolution of heritable resistance.

04
AMR in the Microbiome

Exploring what drives AMR transmission within the human microbiome.

05
Compound discovery

Using streamlined approaches to identify and develop compounds to reduce AMR prevalence.

People

The Lab

A diverse team of microbiologists and bioinformaticians united by curiosity and a shared mission.

Principal Investigator
Dr. Michelle M.C. Buckner

Associate Professor of Antimicrobial Resistance. Michelle completed her PhD at the University of British Columbia, studying Salmonella-host pathogen interactors. Then joined the University of Birmingham as a Post-doc, studying AMR, including Efflux and plasmid-mediated resistance. She established her independent lab in 2019.

Research Fellow
Dr. Rama Bhatia

Rama is interested in microbial evolution and antimicrobial resistance. Her current research explores how biofilm growth influences the evolution of plasmid-mediated AMR and hypervirulence in Klebsiella pneumoniae. She is particularly interested in the interplay between plasmids, host genomes, and environmental factors such as biofilm formation. Originally from India, Rama moved to the UK for her postgraduate training, to further her academic career, completing her PhD at the University of Liverpool. Prior to joining the Buckner lab, she was a postdoctoral researcher at the University of Exeter, where she investigated large-scale phage resistance dynamics in clinical Pseudomonas aeruginosa using integrated genomic and phenotypic datasets.

PhD Candidate
Lauren Bowron

Lauren studies the airway microbiome and its role in respiratory disease, with a particular focus on antimicrobial resistance (AMR) in COPD. Her PhD research explores how AMR develops and spreads within lung bacterial communities, linking genomic data with real-world clinical outcomes. Using an integrative approach that combines whole-genome sequencing, culturomics, and phenotypic analysis, she aims to uncover how resistance emerges, quantify its burden, and identify key bacterial reservoirs driving its transmission.

PhD Candidate
Dorothy Cyril-Okoh

Dorothy studies the evolution of antibiotic-resistant Klebsiella pneumoniae in infection-relevant environments such as medical catheters. Her research investigates how plasmids contribute to resistance persistence, combining experimental evolution with phenotypic assays, genomic analysis and computational modelling. This work is integrated with computational modelling to better understand and predict the long-term behaviour of resistant bacteria.

PhD Candidate
Nneka Igwebuike

Nneka studies how antibiotic resistance genes are transferred between bacteria by conjugation within the human gut microbiome, focusing on Klebsiella pneumoniae. By recreating synthetic gut communities in the lab, her work links specific conditions to changes in conjugation rates and explores ways to inhibit the persistence of these multi‑drug‑resistant strains.
